Workshop: BLINK BUZZ VROOM Circuit #2

The second circuit that we have to build for the day was slightly more complicated. We needed to build a proximity sensor which gives the users a feedback if they are too close in the form of a buzzer noise. The components needed are: mother board, bread board, ultrasonic buzzer, standard buzzer, and wire. What this project does is that it lets out a big noise from the buzzer when you have your fingers covering up the ultrasonic buzzer. The code is pretty easy as me and Sammy successfully got the circuit working and responsing to our hands. This is really fun to do and I think I'm starting to get more comfortable in Physical Computing rather thant just sitting for hours.

WOT is Physical Computing?

Stepping away from the screen and actually build something.
Physical computing involves interacting with the physical world in addition to a screen. It encompasses gathering data from various sensors detecting light, motion, or temperature, and managing devices like motors, speakers, and lights. This approach to computing fosters enjoyment, involvement, and facilitates the comprehension of coding concepts. Arduino and micro:bit emerge as accessible and widely recognized platforms suitable for learners of all ages, offering opportunities to create interactive electronic projects. In this subject particularly, the platform Arduino is used. I was so excited for this week's class regarding physical computing, as it is a whole new world I never explored before. For the next three weeks (from weeks 7-9), we will immerse in the world of Arduino at the Telstra Creator Space, which also serves as a new and fresh learning environment for me.

Hunt'n'Gather #7!

For this week's Hunt'n'Gather, I immersed myself in the world of physical computing. The works I researched were all quite interesting and I love how all of them explored the physical space thoroughly, stepping away from screens. The works I looked at were: Body Sculpture by Jordan Wolfson, Blank Stare in "Paint to Print," and Drawing Machine by Harold Cohen. The most interesting piece is the Blank Stare, where a laser-etching machine draws a pattern on a paper based from the user's brain activity, utiling an Electroencephalogram (EEG). The machine singed the waveform directly & repeatedly into the paper's surface to form a hypnotic, radial pattern.

Watch! Sisters with Transistors

In my free time, I tried watching Sisters with Transistors as recommended in this week's module. Overall, this movie offers an exceptional documentary that empowers the female in a male-lead industry, which is the electronic music industry. It also presents itself as a long-overdue and rarely acknowledged homage to the female composers, musicians, and innovators who have contributed to the relatively brief history of electronic music. The film primarily focuses on approximately nine to ten women within this field, spanning from Clara Rockmore, a pioneering figure in experimental music who mastered the Theremin in elegant evening attire, to Delia Derbyshire, a British composer and mathematician renowned for her co-creation of the iconic Doctor Who theme.

Sisters with Transistors not only chronicles the remarkable achievements of women in electronic music but also serves as an inspiring narrative for the intersection of creativity and technology. The film underscores the innovative spirit and experimental mindset of these artists, many of whom were essentially engaging in early forms of physical computing through their manipulation of electronic sound. This movie inspires me so much especially as a woman myself, to push the boundaries and test the limits of what I believe I can do.

Workshop: WOT's in the Box?

At the start of this week's workshop, we were given a box of electronic kit. We don't know anything at all at the time, not even the name of the device we were working with. A piece of paper were given for us to put the different components on top and try to name them based on what we perceive them to be. This is quite a fun activity as we get to work with our mental models, of what We think the thing is and what it actually does. At the end, we were told the correct names for each component and therefore, we have successfully learned the basics of Arduino.

Workshop: BLINK BUZZ VROOM Circuit #1

For the first workshop experiment, we worked in pairs to get to build a very basic circuit that would make the LED lights blink on and off automatically within the time frame that we have code. The components needed are: motherboard, bread board, LED lights of any color (I chose red), a 220 ohm resistor, and wires, which utilizes very little materials and effort. However, despite the easiness of building this circuit this was still my first Arduino project, which is why I feel so satisfied to see the code and build work along with Sammy, my partner for the day.
