Research for Personal Project

With all the knowledge I've earned and circuits I've built over the past three weeks, I compiled a research of what to do for my final project. I thought about a number of ideas such as: a pill dispenser machine, a candy dispenser machine that gives you candy that matches your shirt color, walking robot that detects lines and moves along the lines, little robot arm that moves left and right, up to an LED machine robot that have feelings and will react to gestures we give them. I decided early on a short while after learning Arduino that I wanted to do physical computing with Arduino for my last project, as I want to step away from the screen and build an immersive experience for people to interact with. My last two options comes down to the color-sorting candy dispenser machine and the LED machine sensitive robot, which I ultimately went for the candy dispenser machine as I want my project to be fun and enjoyable.

Physical Computing: Non-human Drawing Machines

It's all about process, power, and control.
In this era of digitalization, Machines and Artificial Intelligence is taking over humans, from how they do their daily activities to their jobs. This week's seminar delve deeper into Non-human Drawing Machines, which are able to create a whole new "art." However, when a computer is credited with composing an artwork, is the device truly exhibiting "creativity"? Furthermore, can the resulting product genuinely be considered art? Ambiguity surrounds the classification of these artistic works and the creative processes involved. An example we have explored before is in Harold Cohen's AI artmaking program called AARON. A distinctive feature about his machine is that it does not instantly generate images within users input like the usual AI programs we know, but as a live machine, it shows the process of making the image from the very first brush stroke. His device hopes to make people see AI as a tool, not as a solution. That "art" puts its emphasis on the process and became a cumulative body of work that is asking a question.

Hunt'n'Gather #9!

For the last Hunt'n'Gather, we continue to explore the realm of physical computing. I researched a lot about Laura Splan, New York City based visual artist working at the intersections of Science, Technology, and Culture. Her works incorporate a lot of intricate datas such as from an Electroencephalogram (EEG) or an Electromyography (EMG), shown through one of her works "Recursive Expressions" where the EMG data was visualized in the form of a repeated, rotated, and randomly colorized EMG wave forms. Other than that, she also plays with Processing as she built her "autologousReflection" using the software, mainly playing on the webcam function and reflection. Her works really inspired me as someone who is still figuring out what to do with creative coding, and pushes me to think further. Additionally, I also found a buildable Arduino project by Joselyn McDonald called Forget Me Not, which is a remote plant-watering device. Seeing her projects pushed me to brainstorm for my final project idea, in which I also wanted to use Arduino to build a physical device.

Workshop: TOUCH!

In this week's workshop, we set up a circuit with LED lights that will respond within touch by lighting up. The materials used for this were: breadboard, motherboard, LED lights, two types of ohm resistors, and wires. Me and Ileana, my partner for the day successfully build the circuit as the LED lights up when we touch the unconnected end of the wire. We then proceed to experiment using aluminium foils and electrical clips that were able to extend the part where we touch to be larger, as they are very good conductors of electricity. This is a really compelling circuit as I can think about the electrical clips being attached to someone or something (like a piece of clothing or some wearables), and touching them can be a sort of button to light up the LED or to activate something.

Workshop: Hand Sensor Game #1

Rather than building something, for this activity Karen Ann brought a hand sensor with a virtual reality game That can sense the motions of our hands. We get to try and play the game, which for the first one, is about the growth of a flower. The game can go in two different directions:

1. We can pluck each petals of the flower or

2. Rip the whole flower apart

If we chose to pluck each petals, the detached petals will morph into a goldfish swimming around the pond. Otherwise, when we rip the whole flower apart, no goldfish will appear. Both paths lead to the flower being regrown and the cycle continues. The whole class had fun playing with it and it's personally a really memorable experience for me as it is the first time I have ever tried a hand sensor. The device is small and attached with a USB cable, which I am impressed with by how portable and small the device is. It is really intriguing to think about what kind of codes were deployed to make such an intricate device work.

Workshop: Hand Sensor Game #2

Still utilizing the same hand sensor device, other than the flower game there is also this cube stacking game. The objective is to pick up the cubes on the floor and give it to the robots entering the arena, which will become their heads. This game is slightly harder than the other one, but it's interesting to think about the sensor's sentivitiy to movements and the preciseness it tracks.
