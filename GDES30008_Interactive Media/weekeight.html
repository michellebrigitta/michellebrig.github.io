WOT is Speculative Design?

Speculative Design is the act of designing to provoke thought rather than for a practical purpose.

Speculative design involves a design methodology centered on envisioning potential future scenarios and prospects. It serves as a tool for investigating various facets including social, political, technological, and ethical considerations, fostering the generation of innovative ideas and resolutions. Unlike forecasting, speculative design doesn't aim to predict forthcoming events; rather, it delves into diverse possibilities for the future. Through this process, it facilitates a deeper comprehension of the present circumstances and aids in making informed decisions concerning the future.

Hunt'n'Gather #8!

Still in the world of physical computing, I researched immersive artworks that were showcased in exhibitions. For this week's Hunt'n'Gather, the works I researched were: Can't Help Myself by Sun Yuan & Peng Yu, Machine Hallucinations by Nature Dreams, and Remote Entanglements by Laura Splan. I enjoyed all the works, but the most engaging work for me is Machine Hallucinations, which is a giant datasculpture displaying machine-generated, dynanic pigments of nature. The artwork features a synesthetic reality experiments based on GAN algorithms developed by openAI and inspired my fluid dynamics. Nature Dreams, the artist, turns datasets into latent multi-sensory experience to commemorate the beauty of the world we share.

In the Look! Section, we looked at Harold Cohen's Drawing Machine. Being one of the earliest artificial intelligence software, Cohen's AARON is able draws sketches live from the very first stroke. The point that he wants to emphasis with his creation is to make people see AI as a tool and not as a solution. That creative endeavours lies in its journey of process, not in its result.

Workshop of Speculative Objects: Fish Bowl

In this week's Arduino workshop still at the Telstra Creator Space, we continued to broaden our Arduino knowledge by building another circuit, this one called "Fishbowl" in particular. The components needed for this project are: breadboard, motherboard, 10 ohm resistor, a light sensitive sensor, a server motor, and wires. This week, I partnered with Jennifer from Thursday's class to build this circuit, and we successfully are able to make this one work. This immediately became one of my top picks for the most interesting Arduino live demos we've been doing as I can envision a lot of things to do with the server motor: like inserting a designed robot hand on the motor's propellet or to make something move in a certain degree. This links back to the class seminar material of Speculative Design, where the theory is directly applied in my thinking.

Workshop: 8x8 LED Display

This is my favorite personal circuit from this week's class as we jump onto a more complex circuit that requires lots of wires to make the 8x8 LED display work. The materials used were: motherboard, breadboard, wires, 8x8 LED display, and 10 ohm resistors. The code that the LED lights will display are the alphabet A, B, C, D, E, and the numbers 1 until 9. Then, me and my partner modified the code to display our own drawings which is an Among Us mascot. I was really proud in seeing the circuit and code come altogether shown through the display. I can already think of so much things that I can build with the LED light component, such as a machine that gives feedback in the form of emotions shown through the LEDs.
